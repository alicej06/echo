# Executive Summary

---

**Study Overview**

This paper examines how different organizational cultures within the coffee industry affect employee performance and engagement, comparing Starbucks Coffee Company and Dutch Bros Coffee. Using an Issue Analysis approach grounded in organizational behavior theory—including Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory, the Job Characteristics Model, Goal-Setting Theory, the Contingency Approach, and structural versus psychological empowerment frameworks—the analysis investigates why two companies in the same industry, serving similar products with similar labor pools, produce meaningfully divergent workforce outcomes.

---

**Key Findings**

- **Starbucks' "partner" branding creates a gap between espoused values and lived experience.** Despite strong structural empowerment (stock options, tuition reimbursement, healthcare), deficiencies in hygiene factors—inconsistent scheduling, understaffing, and wage stagnation—have fueled unionization at 400+ stores by 2024 and a Glassdoor rating of ~3.7/5.
- **Dutch Bros' culture activates intrinsic motivation more effectively.** Its brotherhood-rooted values, high-energy norms, and the structured "Leadership Pathway" promotion program cultivate psychological empowerment, yielding a ~4.0/5 Glassdoor rating and a 56% turnover rate—well below the 70–80% industry average.
- **Job design matters as much as benefits.** Dutch Bros scores higher on key Job Characteristics Model dimensions—particularly autonomy, task significance, and feedback—giving employees a stronger sense of meaning and ownership in daily work.
- **Starbucks' centralized matrix structure dilutes goal clarity and accountability**, while Dutch Bros' hybrid centralized-ownership/decentralized-culture model aligns more effectively with its young, socially motivated workforce, consistent with the Contingency Approach.
- **Structural empowerment alone is insufficient.** Without psychological empowerment—a felt sense of meaning, competence, self-determination, and impact—extrinsic benefits fail to generate sustained engagement.

---

**Key Recommendations**

- **Starbucks (Short-Term):** Redesign store-level roles to increase autonomy, skill variety, and feedback, shifting from purely structural to psychological empowerment.
- **Starbucks (Short-Term):** Address hygiene factor deficits—predictive scheduling, staffing adequacy, and competitive wages—before expecting motivator-factor investments to improve engagement.
- **Starbucks (Long-Term):** Implement a transparent, SMART goal-based career pathing system with 90-day feedback cycles to rival Dutch Bros' Leadership Pathway.
- **Dutch Bros (Long-Term):** Adopt a Contingency Approach to cultural scaling by establishing regional training hubs, cultural integration teams, and mentorship pairings to preserve its distinctive culture during national expansion.

---

**Main Takeaway**

Competitive benefits and aspirational branding are necessary but insufficient for driving employee engagement. Organizations that embed their values into daily work design, provide clear career progression, and cultivate psychological empowerment—as Dutch Bros demonstrates—achieve stronger retention and engagement outcomes than those relying primarily on structural incentives, regardless of scale or resources.

**Introduction**

The coffee industry in the United States represents one of the most competitive and labor-intensive segments of the broader food and beverage sector. With millions of hourly workers employed across thousands of retail locations, coffee companies face persistent challenges related to employee turnover, engagement, and performance. Two prominent companies operating within this space—Starbucks Coffee Company and Dutch Bros Coffee—offer a compelling comparative case study in how organizational culture shapes the employee experience and, ultimately, operational outcomes. Though both companies sell coffee and operate through retail storefronts staffed predominantly by hourly workers, they differ markedly in their cultural philosophies, organizational structures, and approaches to workforce management. These differences produce measurably divergent outcomes in employee satisfaction, retention, and engagement.

This paper poses the following research question: *How do different organizational cultures within the coffee industry affect overall employee performance and engagement at Starbucks and Dutch Bros?* To answer this question, the paper employs an Issue Analysis lens, which involves identifying a specific organizational problem or phenomenon, examining its root causes through the systematic application of organizational behavior theory, and evaluating the practical implications of the analysis for management practice. Rather than simply describing what each company does, this approach demands an explanation of *why* particular cultural configurations lead to particular employee outcomes, grounded in established academic frameworks.

Several organizational behavior concepts provide the theoretical foundation for this analysis. Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory, which distinguishes between hygiene factors that prevent dissatisfaction and motivator factors that drive genuine engagement, offers a useful framework for evaluating the distinct compensation and recognition strategies employed by each company. The Job Characteristics Model, with its five core dimensions of skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, and feedback, enables a detailed examination of how day-to-day work is designed at each organization and how that design influences intrinsic motivation. Goal-Setting Theory, particularly the emphasis on specific, measurable, attainable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals, provides a lens through which to assess the career development and promotion structures at both firms. Additionally, the concepts of structural and psychological empowerment, the contingency approach to management, and broader organizational culture frameworks—including the role of values, norms, and artifacts—will be applied throughout the analysis to explain the mechanisms linking culture to performance.

Data for this paper is drawn from multiple sources, including Glassdoor employee reviews and ratings, company annual reports and investor filings, journalistic reporting on labor relations and corporate strategy, and publicly available information about each company's organizational policies and programs. By triangulating across these sources, the analysis aims to present a balanced and evidence-based comparison of two distinct cultural models operating within the same industry.

**Summary of Management Topic**

Organizational culture—broadly defined as the shared values, norms, beliefs, and artifacts that characterize how work is conducted and experienced within a firm—is widely recognized in the management literature as a critical determinant of employee performance and engagement. In the coffee industry, where frontline workers serve as the primary interface between the brand and the consumer, the influence of organizational culture is particularly consequential. The way a company structures its operations, communicates its values, recognizes its employees, and provides pathways for growth shapes not only individual job satisfaction but also aggregate outcomes such as turnover rates, productivity, and customer experience quality. Starbucks and Dutch Bros provide instructive cases because they operate in the same industry with similar labor market conditions yet have cultivated meaningfully different organizational cultures—and have produced correspondingly different workforce outcomes.

Starbucks, founded in 1971 and headquartered in Seattle, Washington, is the largest coffeehouse chain in the world, operating more than 35,000 stores globally. The company employs a centralized matrix organizational structure and has long referred to its employees as "partners," a designation intended to signal inclusion and shared ownership. Starbucks offers benefits that are considered above average for the industry, including healthcare coverage, stock options through the Bean Stock program, and tuition reimbursement through a partnership with Arizona State University. Despite these offerings, the company has faced significant workforce challenges in recent years. Employee satisfaction on Glassdoor sits at approximately 3.7 out of 5, and turnover remains high relative to other employers, though consistent with industry norms. Perhaps most notably, Starbucks has experienced a substantial unionization wave, with more than 400 stores voting to unionize by 2024, reflecting widespread employee dissatisfaction with working conditions, scheduling practices, and perceived corporate responsiveness. The company has also undergone leadership transitions, with Brian Niccol recently assuming the role of Chief Executive Officer amid ongoing questions about how to address labor relations and reinvigorate the company's cultural identity.

Dutch Bros Coffee, by contrast, is a comparatively younger and smaller company, founded in 1992 in Grants Pass, Oregon, on values of brotherhood, community, and high-energy customer interaction. The company operates through a centralized ownership model and has cultivated an organizational culture that is frequently described by employees as enthusiastic, tight-knit, and supportive. Dutch Bros maintains a Glassdoor rating of approximately 4.0 out of 5, meaningfully higher than Starbucks. Its reported turnover rate of approximately 56% is notable because, while still substantial in absolute terms, it falls well below the industry average for hourly coffee workers, which is estimated at 70 to 80 percent. A central feature of Dutch Bros' approach to talent management is its "Leadership Pathway" program, a structured promotion pipeline that provides employees with clear, incremental advancement opportunities from entry-level barista positions to leadership and management roles. The company's culture places heavy emphasis on internal promotion and personal development, and its brand identity—both externally facing and internally experienced—is built around energy, positivity, and relational connection.

These facts establish the empirical foundation for the analysis that follows. Both companies invest in their workforces, yet the nature, structure, and cultural context of those investments differ in ways that correlate with divergent employee outcomes. The subsequent sections of this paper will apply organizational behavior theory to explain the mechanisms underlying these differences, examining how the specific cultural and structural choices made by each company either enhance or undermine employee motivation, engagement, and performance.

**Analysis Part 1: Organizational Culture and Motivation at Starbucks and Dutch Bros**

**The "Partner" Promise: Starbucks' Cultural Aspirations and Realities**

Starbucks has long positioned itself as a progressive employer in the quick-service industry, branding every employee—from entry-level barista to corporate executive—as a "partner." This linguistic choice is a deliberate cultural artifact, one that, according to organizational culture frameworks, signals values of egalitarianism, shared ownership, and mutual respect (Schein, 2010). On paper, the partner model suggests that Starbucks employees are stakeholders in the company's mission, reinforced by tangible benefits such as stock options (Bean Stock), tuition reimbursement through the Starbucks College Achievement Plan, and health insurance for part-time workers. These elements constitute what Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory classifies as hygiene factors—extrinsic conditions that, when adequate, prevent dissatisfaction but do not inherently generate motivation (Herzberg, 1959). Starbucks has historically excelled in offering hygiene factors that exceed industry norms for hourly food-service work, and these benefits have been central to its employer brand.

However, a significant gap has emerged between Starbucks' espoused values and its enacted culture. The wave of unionization that swept through more than 400 Starbucks locations by 2024 provides compelling evidence that hygiene factors alone are insufficient and that several of them may, in fact, be deteriorating. Workers organizing under Starbucks Workers United have cited inconsistent scheduling, understaffing, stagnating wages relative to workload increases, and a perceived erosion of managerial respect as primary grievances (Wiener-Bronner, 2023). Through Herzberg's lens, these complaints map directly onto deficient hygiene factors: working conditions, company policies, supervision, and salary. When employees feel that even the baseline conditions of their work are inadequate, dissatisfaction becomes pervasive regardless of how aspirational the company's stated culture may be. The "partner" label, once a source of cultural pride, has become a point of irony for many baristas who feel that the title promises a level of voice and agency that the centralized matrix organizational structure does not deliver. Starbucks' Glassdoor rating of approximately 3.7 out of 5 reflects this tension—adequate but unremarkable, suggesting a workforce that is not overtly hostile but is far from deeply engaged.

Equally important is what Herzberg's framework reveals about the motivator side of the equation at Starbucks. Motivator factors—those that drive genuine satisfaction and intrinsic motivation—include recognition, achievement, responsibility, opportunities for growth, and the meaningfulness of the work itself. While Starbucks offers tuition benefits and periodic recognition programs, the day-to-day experience of a barista in a high-volume store is often characterized by repetitive task execution under time pressure, with limited autonomy and infrequent meaningful feedback from supervisors. The nature of the work, in other words, does not consistently activate motivator factors, leaving employees dependent on extrinsic hygiene factors that are themselves under strain.

**Brotherhood on the Floor: Dutch Bros' Culture as a Motivational Engine**

Dutch Bros presents a strikingly different cultural profile. Founded by brothers Dane and Travis Boersma in Grants Pass, Oregon, the company's organizational culture is rooted in brotherhood, community, and high-energy positivity—values that permeate its daily operations rather than residing primarily in corporate messaging. Employees, referred to as "broistas," are expected to embody enthusiasm, genuine connection with customers, and mutual support for fellow team members. These cultural norms are reinforced through visible artifacts: upbeat music at drive-through windows, enthusiastic greetings, and a hiring process that explicitly prioritizes attitude and relational energy over technical coffee knowledge. According to organizational culture frameworks, Dutch Bros' culture reflects strong alignment between its espoused values and the observable behaviors on the floor, creating a sense of cultural coherence that employees can internalize authentically.

The Job Characteristics Model (JCM), developed by Hackman and Oldham (1976), provides a useful framework for comparing how the two companies structure work and, consequently, how their cultures translate into psychological engagement. The JCM identifies five core job dimensions—skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, and feedback—that collectively determine the motivating potential of a given role.

At Starbucks, **skill variety** is moderate; baristas learn an extensive menu and operate complex equipment, but the standardized nature of drink preparation can become routine over time. **Task identity** is relatively high in that a barista often completes a drink from start to finish, yet during peak periods, task segmentation (one person on register, another on bar) can fragment this experience. **Task significance** is moderate; baristas understand they contribute to customers' daily routines, but the transactional pace of a busy store can diminish the felt meaningfulness of individual interactions. **Autonomy** is low within Starbucks' centralized matrix structure, which dictates recipes, procedures, store layouts, and even the language employees use to communicate with customers. Finally, **feedback** is inconsistent; while formal performance reviews exist, the day-to-day recognition from shift supervisors varies enormously by location, and the unionization movement suggests that many employees feel unheard by management.

Dutch Bros scores comparatively higher across several JCM dimensions. **Skill variety** is enhanced by the expectation that broistas are not merely drink-makers but relationship-builders; each customer interaction requires social creativity and emotional intelligence alongside technical beverage skills. **Task identity** remains similar to Starbucks in the literal act of drink preparation, but the cultural emphasis on owning the customer experience from greeting to farewell gives employees a stronger sense of completing a meaningful whole. **Task significance** is elevated by Dutch Bros' community-oriented mission and frequent involvement in local charitable events, which help employees connect their daily work to a broader purpose. **Autonomy** is notably higher despite Dutch Bros operating under a centralized ownership model; broistas are encouraged to personalize interactions, improvise positive gestures for customers, and exercise judgment in creating a memorable experience—a form of psychological empowerment that grants felt ownership even within structural constraints. **Feedback** is frequent and culturally embedded; the high-energy environment normalizes immediate positive reinforcement from peers and supervisors, and the company's "Leadership Pathway" program provides structured, ongoing feedback tied to career development milestones.

**Intrinsic Motivation and the Leadership Pathway**

The distinction between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation is essential to understanding why Dutch Bros achieves a 56% turnover rate—meaningfully below the industry average of 70–80% for hourly coffee workers—while Starbucks continues to struggle with high turnover despite offering objectively competitive extrinsic benefits. Extrinsic motivation, driven by external rewards such as pay, benefits, and stock options, can attract and retain employees to a point, but extensive research in organizational behavior demonstrates that intrinsic motivation—derived from the inherent interest, enjoyment, and meaningfulness of the work itself—is a stronger predictor of sustained engagement and discretionary effort (Deci & Ryan, 1985).

Dutch Bros' Leadership Pathway program exemplifies how an organization can systematically cultivate intrinsic motivation. The program provides a clearly articulated promotion track from entry-level broista through shift lead, assistant manager, operator, and eventually franchise ownership. Critically, this pathway functions not merely as an extrinsic incentive (higher pay at each level) but as a motivator factor in Herzberg's framework: it offers growth, achievement, increased responsibility, and recognition. Employees pursuing the Leadership Pathway report a sense of personal development and forward momentum that transforms daily work from a transactional job into a stage in a meaningful career narrative. This structure also aligns with intrinsic motivation by satisfying what Self-Determination Theory identifies as core psychological needs: competence (skill-building at each level), autonomy (increasing decision-making authority), and relatedness (deepening ties to the Dutch Bros community) (Deci & Ryan, 1985).

At Starbucks, comparable advancement opportunities exist but are less culturally salient. The path from barista to shift supervisor to assistant store manager is available, yet employees frequently report that promotions feel bureaucratic, inconsistent, and contingent on factors outside their control—perceptions exacerbated by the scale of a company operating over 16,000 U.S. locations. The arrival of new CEO Brian Niccol signals corporate awareness that cultural and motivational recalibration is needed, but whether structural changes will follow remains to be seen. ## Analysis Part 2: Management Structures and Their Effect on Employee Performance

### Centralized Authority and the Accountability Gap at Starbucks

Starbucks operates under a centralized matrix structure in which employees—referred to as "partners"—report to multiple layers of management spanning store-level supervisors, district managers, and regional directors. While this structure is designed to maintain brand consistency across more than 16,000 U.S. locations, it simultaneously introduces significant challenges for individual accountability and employee autonomy. When analyzed through the lens of **Goal-Setting Theory**, which emphasizes the importance of specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals paired with clear accountability mechanisms (Locke & Latham, 2002), the matrix structure dilutes the goal-setting process. Partners often receive directives from corporate headquarters that are standardized across all locations, leaving little room for store-level managers to tailor performance objectives to local contexts. The result is a disconnect between corporate-level strategy and individual employee goal ownership. Without personalized, meaningful goals and direct accountability to a single clear authority, partners may experience ambiguity in expectations—a condition that Goal-Setting Theory identifies as detrimental to both motivation and performance. The recent unionization wave, which saw over 400 stores organize by 2024, further illustrates this structural tension; partners sought collective bargaining not merely for wage increases but for a greater voice in workplace decision-making, signaling that the existing management structure failed to provide adequate channels for feedback and participatory goal setting.

### Dutch Bros' Contingency-Based Structural Advantage

Dutch Bros, by contrast, operates under a centralized ownership model that paradoxically fosters a decentralized, high-energy culture at the store level. This seeming contradiction is best understood through the **Contingency Approach** to management, which holds that no single organizational structure is universally optimal; rather, effectiveness depends on the alignment between structural design and situational variables such as workforce demographics, organizational size, and strategic objectives (Donaldson, 2001). Dutch Bros' workforce is predominantly young, socially motivated, and drawn to a fast-paced environment rooted in the company's founding values of brotherhood and community. The centralized ownership ensures consistency in brand identity and operational standards, while the decentralized cultural norms empower individual employees—called "broistas"—to bring authentic energy, personality, and customer connection to every interaction. The Contingency Approach explains why this hybrid model succeeds for Dutch Bros but would be difficult to replicate at Starbucks: Dutch Bros is a smaller, younger organization whose workforce self-selects into a culture of enthusiasm and relational engagement, whereas Starbucks' sheer scale and diverse workforce require more formalized coordination mechanisms that inevitably constrain autonomy. The contingency variables differ so fundamentally that the same structural approach would yield divergent outcomes in each organization.

### Turnover Rates as Structural Performance Outcomes

These structural differences manifest concretely in employee turnover, one of the most reliable indicators of organizational performance in the service industry. Dutch Bros reports a turnover rate of approximately 56%, which, while substantial in absolute terms, falls well below the coffee industry average of 70–80% for hourly workers. Starbucks, despite its "partner" branding and more extensive benefits package, experiences turnover at or above industry averages. This disparity is not easily explained by compensation alone; rather, it reflects the structural environments in which employees operate. Dutch Bros' **"Leadership Pathway"** program provides clear upward mobility through a structured promotion pipeline that aligns with Goal-Setting Theory's emphasis on specific, progressive objectives and visible accountability. Employees can see a tangible trajectory from entry-level broista to shift lead to operator, creating sustained motivation through incremental goal attainment. Starbucks' matrix structure, while offering some advancement opportunities, often obscures the path forward, as promotion decisions involve multiple managerial inputs and corporate-level approvals that feel distant and opaque to frontline workers.

### Structural Versus Psychological Empowerment

The concept of empowerment further clarifies these divergent outcomes. Starbucks primarily relies on **structural empowerment**—providing formal benefits such as healthcare, tuition reimbursement through the Arizona State University partnership, and stock options (Bean Stock). These are tangible, policy-driven mechanisms that grant employees access to resources and opportunities. However, structural empowerment alone does not guarantee engagement if employees lack a felt sense of influence over their daily work. **Psychological empowerment**, defined as an intrinsic sense of meaning, competence, self-determination, and impact in one's role (Spreitzer, 1995), appears more strongly cultivated at Dutch Bros, where the culture explicitly encourages individual expression, peer recognition, and autonomous customer engagement. Broistas are empowered not just through formal programs but through a daily experience of mattering—to their teams, their customers, and the broader organizational mission.

### Glassdoor Ratings as Engagement Indicators

Glassdoor ratings serve as a useful, if imperfect, proxy for employee engagement across the two companies. Dutch Bros holds an approximate rating of 4.0 out of 5, compared to Starbucks' 3.7 out of 5. While the difference may appear modest numerically, it reflects a meaningful gap in employee sentiment at scale and aligns with the structural and cultural dynamics discussed above. Dutch Bros employees consistently cite culture, camaraderie, and growth opportunities in their reviews—factors tied to psychological empowerment and intrinsic motivation. Starbucks reviews, while acknowledging strong benefits, frequently reference understaffing, corporate rigidity, and limited autonomy—concerns rooted directly in the constraints of its centralized matrix structure. These patterns reinforce the conclusion that management structure is not merely an operational consideration but a fundamental driver of employee engagement and, ultimately, organizational performance.

**Recommendations and Lessons Learned**

The preceding analysis reveals that Starbucks and Dutch Bros occupy distinct positions along the spectrum of organizational culture, employee engagement, and performance outcomes. While both companies have achieved remarkable commercial success in the specialty coffee industry, their divergent approaches to culture-building, empowerment, and motivation yield meaningfully different results in employee satisfaction and retention. Drawing on the organizational behavior theories applied throughout this paper, the following recommendations offer specific, actionable strategies that each company can adopt to strengthen the relationship between organizational culture and employee performance. Because Starbucks demonstrates greater room for improvement—evidenced by its higher turnover rates, lower Glassdoor ratings (approximately 3.7 out of 5), and the groundswell of unionization across more than 400 stores by 2024—the majority of these recommendations are directed toward that organization. However, Dutch Bros faces its own challenges as it scales rapidly from a regional chain to a national brand, and targeted recommendations address how it can preserve its distinctive culture during that transition.

**Recommendation 1 (Starbucks – Short-Term): Redesign Store-Level Roles to Enhance Psychological Empowerment and Intrinsic Motivation**

Starbucks should undertake an immediate audit of store-level job responsibilities with the explicit goal of enriching barista roles along the five core dimensions identified in Hackman and Oldham's Job Characteristics Model. At present, Starbucks' centralized matrix structure, while efficient for maintaining brand consistency across more than 16,000 U.S. locations, tends to standardize tasks to such a degree that employees experience limited skill variety, reduced autonomy, and minimal task identity. A barista who follows rigidly prescribed drink recipes, adheres to corporate planograms for merchandise displays, and has no input into scheduling or workflow design is unlikely to experience the three critical psychological states—experienced meaningfulness, experienced responsibility, and knowledge of results—that the Job Characteristics Model identifies as essential drivers of internal work motivation.

Concretely, Starbucks should empower shift supervisors and baristas to make localized decisions about product presentation, community engagement activities, and customer service recovery within clearly defined parameters. This change would shift the company's empowerment model from one that is primarily structural—where "partner" status and stock options provide formal mechanisms of inclusion—toward one that also cultivates genuine psychological empowerment, defined as employees' felt sense of meaning, competence, self-determination, and impact. Research in organizational behavior consistently demonstrates that psychological empowerment mediates the relationship between organizational practices and key outcomes such as job satisfaction, organizational commitment, and discretionary effort. By granting baristas greater decision-making latitude at the point of customer interaction, Starbucks can bolster intrinsic motivation without incurring significant additional costs.

**Recommendation 2 (Starbucks – Short-Term): Address Hygiene Factor Deficits Before Investing Further in Motivator Factors**

The unionization wave sweeping through Starbucks locations offers a clear diagnostic signal when interpreted through the lens of Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory. Unionization drives are most commonly triggered not by the absence of motivator factors—such as recognition, growth opportunities, or meaningful work—but by acute dissatisfaction with hygiene factors, including wages, working conditions, scheduling predictability, and perceptions of fair treatment by management. When hygiene factors fall below employees' threshold of acceptability, no amount of motivator-factor investment (e.g., the "partner" branding, tuition reimbursement through the Starbucks College Achievement Plan, or Bean Stock equity grants) can compensate for the underlying dissatisfaction.

Under new CEO Brian Niccol's leadership, Starbucks should prioritize a comprehensive review of compensation competitiveness, scheduling transparency, and staffing adequacy at unionized and non-unionized stores alike. Specific actions should include implementing predictive scheduling practices that give baristas at least two weeks' advance notice of shifts, establishing a minimum guaranteed weekly hour threshold for part-time partners, and conducting market-rate wage analyses on a biannual rather than annual basis. Only once these hygiene factors are adequately addressed will Starbucks' considerable investments in motivator factors—career development programs, recognition initiatives, and purpose-driven branding—yield their intended effects on engagement and performance. Herzberg's theory is unambiguous on this point: hygiene factors do not motivate when present, but they profoundly demotivate when absent.

**Recommendation 3 (Starbucks – Long-Term): Implement a Formalized Goal-Setting and Career Pathing System Modeled on SMART Goal Principles**

One of Dutch Bros' most notable cultural advantages is its Leadership Pathway program, which provides employees with a transparent, structured trajectory from entry-level broista to shop operator and beyond. Starbucks, despite its vastly greater resources, lacks an equivalently visible and systematically communicated promotion framework at the store level. Goal-Setting Theory, as articulated by Locke and Latham, demonstrates that specific and challenging goals, accompanied by feedback and commitment mechanisms, produce significantly higher performance than vague aspirations or no goals at all.

Starbucks should develop and implement a career pathing system that sets SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) milestones for baristas aspiring to shift supervisor, assistant store manager, and store manager roles. Each milestone should be paired with clearly articulated competency requirements, estimated timelines for progression, and structured feedback check-ins at 90-day intervals. Critically, this system should incorporate the accountability dimension emphasized by Goal-Setting Theory by assigning district managers explicit responsibility for conducting development conversations and tracking promotion pipeline metrics. By making career advancement tangible and measurable rather than opaque and discretionary, Starbucks can enhance the motivator factors of achievement, advancement, and growth that Herzberg identifies as the true drivers of workplace satisfaction—while simultaneously giving employees a reason to remain with the organization long enough to realize those goals, thereby reducing the costly turnover that currently plagues its operations.

**Recommendation 4 (Dutch Bros – Long-Term): Adopt a Contingency Approach to Cultural Scaling as the Organization Expands Nationally**

Dutch Bros' high-energy, brotherhood-rooted culture has been a decisive competitive advantage, contributing to a Glassdoor rating of approximately 4 out of 5 and a turnover rate of roughly 56 percent—well below the industry average of 70 to 80 percent for hourly coffee workers. However, as the company expands beyond its Pacific Northwest and Sun Belt strongholds into new geographic markets, it faces the classic scaling dilemma: the cultural practices that thrive in a tightly knit, regionally concentrated organization may not transfer seamlessly to a dispersed, nationally distributed one.

The Contingency Approach to management holds that no single organizational strategy or cultural practice is universally optimal; rather, effectiveness depends on the alignment between practices and situational variables such as organizational size, environmental complexity, workforce demographics, and market conditions. Dutch Bros should proactively adopt this framework by establishing a Cultural Integration Team tasked with assessing how core cultural artifacts (e.g., the high-energy drink-calling rituals, the "bro-ista" identity, the community engagement expectations) are received in new markets and by new employee demographics who may not share the founders' Pacific Northwest cultural context. Additionally, Dutch Bros should invest in regional training hubs that can adapt onboarding and leadership development practices to local norms while preserving the organization's core values of speed, positivity, and community.

To safeguard its promotion-from-within model during rapid expansion, Dutch Bros should also formalize knowledge transfer mechanisms—such as mentorship pairings between veteran operators and newly promoted leaders in expansion markets—to ensure that the tacit cultural knowledge embedded in the Leadership Pathway is not diluted as the organization grows. Without deliberate attention to contingency factors, Dutch Bros risks experiencing the cultural erosion that has afflicted many high-growth restaurant and retail brands, ultimately undermining the very employee engagement and performance advantages that fuel its current success.

**Conclusion**

This analysis set out to examine how different organizational cultures within the coffee industry affect overall employee performance and engagement, using Starbucks and Dutch Bros as comparative cases. The findings reveal that while both companies espouse employee-centric values, the mechanisms through which those values are enacted—and the degree to which they translate into genuine empowerment, motivation, and satisfaction—differ substantially.

Starbucks has built an impressive infrastructure of structural empowerment, offering equity participation, tuition benefits, and the symbolic designation of employees as "partners." Yet its centralized matrix structure, high standardization of tasks, and persistent deficits in hygiene factors such as scheduling predictability and wage competitiveness have generated significant employee dissatisfaction, as reflected in its comparatively lower Glassdoor rating and the rapid spread of unionization.
